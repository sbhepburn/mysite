Git, Finally.

You know the commands. You've typed rebase a hundred times with your eyes half-closed. This is the part nobody handed you: the map.

THE ONLY IDEA YOU NEED

Git is a rail network. Every commit is a station — a complete, frozen photograph of your whole project, welded permanently into the map. A branch is a nameplate pinned to exactly one station. HEAD is the you-are-here marker. Every git command, however scary its name, is one of two moves: weld a new station, or slide a nameplate. Ride the line below and watch.

02

Commits — the stations

The single biggest misconception in git, corrected in one sentence:

THE METAPHOR

A commit is not a diff. It's a complete photograph of your entire project — a whole station, welded into the map with a serial number (the hash) stamped from its contents plus its parent's serial number. That chaining is why history is tamper-evident: change anything in any old station and every serial number after it stops matching. Diffs are something git computes on demand by comparing two photographs.

WHY YOU'D WANT THIS

Because stations never change, time travel is safe. Commit early and often — dense stations mean you can step back to exactly the moment before things went sideways. A commit costs almost nothing; a missing one costs an afternoon.

THE CLASSIC GOTCHA, DEFUSED

--amend doesn't edit a station. It welds a replacement station with a new serial number and quietly abandons the old one. Harmless on your own track — but it's why amending a pushed commit makes teammates' maps disagree with yours.

03

Street → Platform → Train

Why does staging even exist? Because it's the platform — the one place where you compose exactly what boards the next train.

THE METAPHOR

Your working directory is the street: live, messy, everything happening at once. git add walks a change down to the platform (the staging area / index). git commit is the train departing — only what's on the platform boards, and a new station gets welded behind you. Staging exists so a chaotic hour of street work can become three clean, single-purpose commits instead of one commit called "stuff".

THE CLASSIC GOTCHA, DEFUSED

"I added the file, then committed, and it still shows as modified?!" — You edited it again after it boarded. The platform holds the version as it was at add time. That's also the difference between git diff (street vs platform) and git diff --staged (platform vs last station).

04

Branches & HEAD — nameplates, not copies

If you unlearn one thing today: a branch is not a folder, not a copy, not a parallel codebase.

THE METAPHOR

A branch is a magnetic nameplate pinned to exactly one station — 41 bytes on disk, literally a file containing one hash. The colored "line" you see on maps is just drawn by following parents backwards from wherever the plate sits. Commit, and the plate slides forward onto the new station. HEAD is one level up: it's the you-are-here marker, and it usually points at a nameplate, not a station.

WHY YOU'D WANT THIS

Because nameplates are free, make one for everything — every experiment, every "let me just try". Nothing is copied, nothing is slow. Deleting a merged branch deletes a sticker, not work.

"DETACHED HEAD" IS NOT AN ERROR

Checking out an old hash makes HEAD point at a station directly, with no nameplate. You got off the train to sightsee — totally fine. The only rule: if you start laying new track from here and want to keep it, pin a plate on it with git switch -c rescue. Otherwise the track goes unnamed (and even then, the reflog remembers it for weeks).

05

Merge vs Rebase — junction or re-laid track

Same starting map, two ways to bring lines together. Toggle and compare.

Two lines diverged at B: main built C while you built D–E. Now what?

Merge builds a junction station with two parents. Nothing existing moves or changes — history stays exactly as it happened, at the cost of one extra station and a wigglier map. Always safe, everywhere.

Rebase picks up your track and re-lays it starting from main's tip. Clean, linear map — but D and E were rebuilt as D′ and E′ with new hashes; the originals are abandoned. That's the whole danger in one image.

THE ONE RULE

Re-lay your own unshared track as much as you like. Never rebase track other people are already riding — after you push, their maps point at stations that no longer exist on yours. Private branch: rebase for a tidy story. Shared branch: merge.

06

Stash — the luggage locker

THE METAPHOR

You're mid-change on the street, and suddenly you need to switch lines now — an urgent bug, a review, a demo. git stash sweeps your uncommitted street mess into a locker at the station, leaving the street spotless. Ride wherever you need; come back and pop the locker — even onto a different line.

WHY YOU'D WANT THIS

It's the answer to git refusing to switch branches because "your local changes would be overwritten." Stash, switch, do the thing, switch back, pop. Ten seconds, zero fear.

TWO GOTCHAS, DEFUSED

1. Brand-new files don't get swept up by default — they're not tracked yet. Use -u. 2. The lockers are a stack (stash@{0}, stash@{1}…). Don't hoard; a week-old anonymous locker is a mystery box. If it matters, commit it to a branch instead — commits are named, stashes aren't.

07

Cherry-pick — photocopy one station

THE METAPHOR

cherry-pick photocopies the change one station introduced on another line and welds the copy onto yours. It's not the same station — the copy gets its own serial number. The classic use: a critical fix landed on main, and the release-2.4 line needs exactly that fix and nothing else around it.

THE GOTCHA, DEFUSED

A copy is not shared history. Git doesn't know D′ "is" D, so if the two lines fully merge later you can hit déjà-vu conflicts. Cherry-pick when you want one surgical change; merge when you want the whole line. If you're cherry-picking five commits, you probably want a merge.

09

Revert — build the anti-station

THE METAPHOR

revert doesn't touch the past at all. It welds a brand-new station whose change exactly cancels an old one — history moves only forward, and the map everyone shares stays intact. Reset rewrites the line; revert extends it with an undo.

THE DECISION RULE

Private track → reset. Nobody's riding it; sculpt freely. Shared track → revert. The bad commit is already in a hundred clones; erasing it from yours fixes nothing and breaks everyone. This one rule replaces most "reset vs revert" googling forever.

10

Reflog — the stationmaster's ledger

THE METAPHOR

Every single time your you-are-here marker moves — commit, switch, reset, rebase, merge, everything — the stationmaster writes it in a local ledger. Abandoned stations, "deleted" branches, pre-rebase originals: their addresses are all in there, kept for roughly 90 days. git reflog is why the true answer to "I destroyed everything" is almost always "no you didn't."

THE RESCUE, IN TWO LINES

Find the moment before disaster in the ledger, then either jump your line back (git reset --hard HEAD@{2}) or pin a plate on the lost station (git branch rescue <hash>). Once you can do this cold, destructive commands stop being scary — and git gets fun.

ONE HONEST LIMIT

The ledger only records where your marker has been, on your machine. It can't recover street changes that were never committed, and it doesn't exist on the remote. It's a safety net, not a time machine for things git never saw.

origin isn't "the real repo." It's just another city holding its own full copy of the network.

YOUR CITY

main → station  E (your line)
origin/main →  D (a photo, possibly stale)

origin/main is a bookmark: "their main, last time I looked." It is not live.

THE THREE VERBS, UNTANGLED

fetch = take a fresh photo of their city. Updates your origin/* bookmarks and touches nothing of yours — always safe, run it constantly. pull = fetch and then merge (or rebase) their new track into your line — two moves wearing one trenchcoat. push = mail your new track to their city; rejected if their line moved since your last photo, which just means: fetch first and reconcile.

PREDICT THE MAP

You run git fetch. What changes in your working files?

FORCE PUSH = DEMOLISHING STATIONS IN A SHARED CITY

After rewriting local history, plain push gets rejected — that's git protecting your teammates. If the branch is truly yours alone, use --force-with-lease: it aborts if someone else pushed since your last fetch. Bare --force has no such seatbelt.

12

Pull requests — the city notice board

Surprise: git pull-request isn't a command. PRs aren't git at all.

THE METAPHOR

A pull request is a proposal pinned to the notice board of the hosting city (GitHub, GitLab): "I'd like to connect my line to Main — here's the track, please inspect it." The review threads, approvals, and CI checks are a conversation layer the host adds on top of plain branches. What actually happens to the commits is decided by which green button gets pressed:

Junction on Main. Your stations C and D join Main's map exactly as you built them, plus a merge commit. Full history, wiggly map. Best when the branch's internal story matters.

Your whole branch becomes one brand-new station S on Main. Beautifully linear — and it's why your commits seem to "disappear" after squash-merging: C and D were never placed on Main. Delete the branch guilt-free; don't keep building on it, or the next PR re-proposes old track.

Copies of your stations re-laid onto Main's tip. Linear like squash but keeps individual commits — as C′ and D′, with new serial numbers. Middle ground for teams that want clean history and commit granularity.

WHY THIS MATTERS DAY-TO-DAY

Half of all "git is haunted" moments after a PR merges — dangling branches, "already up to date" lies, phantom re-conflicts — come from not knowing which button your team presses. Find out once; the haunting stops.

13

Tags — the commemorative plaque

THE METAPHOR

A tag is a bronze plaque bolted to one station, forever: v2.1.0 was this exact snapshot. Nameplates (branches) slide forward with every commit; plaques never move. That's the entire difference, and it's why releases are tagged, not branched.

THE GOTCHA, DEFUSED

Tags don't travel with a normal push — your teammates won't see v2.1.0 until you push it explicitly. Also: prefer annotated tags (-a); they store who, when, and why. Lightweight tags are just a bare pointer.

Bisect — the track inspector

"It worked two weeks ago, it's broken now, there are 300 commits in between." Don't read 300 commits. Binary-search them.

THE METAPHOR

Tell the inspector one good station and one bad station. She rides to the midpoint, you test, you say good or bad, and half the suspects are eliminated. Repeat. A thousand stations take ten rides. Try it — one of these 16 stations secretly broke the build:

The inspector is waiting. (You'd run git bisect start, then mark one bad and one good commit.)

THE POWER MOVE

git bisect run ./test.sh automates the whole hunt: git checks out midpoints and runs your script until it names the exact guilty commit while you get coffee. If you've never used it, this is the single highest-leverage git feature you're not using.

15

The panic desk

The five most common "oh no" moments, each with the calm fix. Bookmark this stop.

I committed to the wrong branch

The station exists; it just needs a different nameplate. If the right branch doesn't exist yet: git switch -c right-branch — done, the commit rides with you. If it does exist: note the hash, then git switch right-branch && git cherry-pick <hash>, then go back and git reset --hard HEAD~1 on the wrong line to slide its plate back.

I reset --hard and lost work

If it was ever committed: git reflog, find the address from before the reset, git reset --hard HEAD@{1} (or git branch rescue <hash>). The station was abandoned, never demolished. If it was never committed, the ledger never saw it — this is the one wound git can't heal, and the argument for committing early.

A bad commit is already pushed

Don't rewrite shared track. Weld the anti-station: git revert <hash>, push. History stays intact, the bug is gone, nobody's clone breaks. Reset-and-force-push on a shared branch turns one person's mistake into everyone's morning.

Merge conflict, everything is angry

First: you can always bail — git merge --abort (or rebase --abort) returns you exactly to before. The <<<<<<< markers just mean two stations photographed the same lines differently and git won't guess. Edit each file to the version you want, git add it (that's how you tell git "resolved"), then git merge --continue. It's tedious, not dangerous.

I committed a secret (or a 2GB file)

Not pushed yet? git commit --amend (or reset, fix, recommit) — the old station is abandoned before anyone saw it. Already pushed? Rotate the secret first — assume it's compromised; scrubbing history doesn't un-leak it. Then rewrite with git filter-repo and coordinate the force-push with your team, because every clone must re-fetch the rebuilt map.

16

The whole model in one paragraph

Git is a rail network of stations — immutable snapshots chained by parent hashes. A branch is a nameplate pinned to one station that slides forward as you commit; HEAD is the you-are-here marker, and pointing it at a bare station is just sightseeing. Changes walk from the street to the platform and depart as a new station. Merge builds a junction; rebase re-lays your track as fresh copies — fine on private lines, rude on shared ones. Reset slides your plate backward (the dial only decides what happens to your luggage); revert welds a forward-moving anti-station instead. Remotes are other cities, and origin/main is only your last photo of theirs — fetch refreshes the photo, pull also merges, push mails your track. A tag is a plaque that never moves, a PR is a proposal on the host's notice board, bisect binary-searches the line, and the reflog remembers everywhere you've ever stood — which is why, in git, almost nothing committed is ever truly lost.

That's the map. You can throw away the cheat sheet now — or better: scroll back up and break the playground a few more times until sliding nameplates feels boring. Boring is mastery. 🚇
